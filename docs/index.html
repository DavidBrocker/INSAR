Observed Relations between Intra-Individual and Real-World Outcomes in a Sample of Autistic and Non-Autistic Youth

Background

  • Research has begun to characterize the relations between intra-individual and “real word” outcomes for autistic and non-autistic adults (Morrison et al. 2020).

  • However, very little research has directly linked these levels of analysis for autistic youth.

  • An understanding of these relations is crucial for the development of precise and ecologically valid social intervention programs for autistic youth.

Objective

To examine the relations between emotion recognition, mental state understanding, and ecologically valid measures of social behavior in a sample of autistic and non-autistic youth.

Method

  • Autism feature levels were assessed using the Autism Diagnostic Observation Schedule (ADOS-2), (Lord et al. 2012) Comparison Score (CS), (Gotham, Pickles, and Lord 2009; Hus and Lord 2014)

  • Facial emotion recognition (FER) and vocal emotion recognition (VER) were measured using the Diagnostic Analysis of Nonverbal Accuracy, volume 2 (DANVA-2) (Nowicki and Duke 2008).

  • Theory of Mind (ToM) was assessed using the SELWeb measure of social cognition (Russo-Ponsaran et al. 2019).

  • Proportion of reciprocated friendships was measured using peer-rated sociometric procedures (Coie, Dodge, and Coppotelli 1982) administered by primary teachers in a classroom setting.

  • Positive, negative, and low-level peer interaction behaviors were measured by trained coders following a peer interaction assessment (McNair et al. 2023).

Conclusions

  • Results suggest a more complex and subtle relationship between emotion recognition, mental state understanding, and social outcomes than has been previously reported, and which differs between autistic and non- autistic youth.

  • While emotion recognition (particularly vocal) correlated with social outcomes more strongly for some outcome measures, the overall pattern of effects suggest that ToM (mental state understanding) was more consistently related to outcomes across measures in the autistic sample, albeit not always in a positive direction.

  • Present findings not only better elucidate the relation between intra-individual variables and “real world” social outcomes for autistic youth, but also identify promising targets for the development of individualized social intervention programs.

Conference Abstract

Background: Research has begun to characterize the relations between intra-individual

and “real world” outcomes for autistic and non-autistic adults (Morrison et al., 2020).

However, very little research has directly linked these levels of analysis for autistic

youth. An understanding of these relations is crucial for the development of precise

and ecologically valid social intervention programs for autistic youth.

Objectives: To examine the relations between emotion recognition, mental state

understanding, and ecologically valid measures of social behavior in a sample of autistic

and non-autistic youth.

Methods: One hundred and sixty-seven autistic (n= 86) and non-autistic (n= 81) youth

completed standardized emotion recognition, Theory of Mind (ToM), and peer

interaction tasks (Table 1). Sociometric-ratings by peers were collected in the

classroom. Autism symptom levels were assessed using the Autism Diagnostic

Observation Schedule (ADOS-2; Lord et al., 2012). Correlations between intra-individual

variables (emotion recognition, ToM) and social outcomes (autism symptom level, peer

interaction behaviors, sociometric-ratings) were examined.

Results: In the autistic sample, facial emotion recognition (r=.23, p<.05) and vocal

emotion recognition (r=.33, p<.01) correlated with autism symptom severity. ToM

correlated with autism symptom severity at a similar magnitude (r=-.24, p<.05).

Meanwhile, facial emotion recognition (r=-.25, p<.05) correlated with positive peer

interaction during unstructured interaction with greater verbal demands; vocal emotion

recognition (r=.25, p<.05) correlated with low-level peer interaction during this same

task. ToM correlated m or e s t r on gl y with positive interaction (r=.32, p<.01) during this

same task. Of these tasks, only ToM correlated with more over al l positive behavior

across all tasks (r=.25, p<.05), but with f ewer reciprocated friendships in the classroom

(r=-.56, p<.05).

For the non-autistic sample, comparative strength and direction of relations was mixed.

Relation to autism symptom severity was in the same direction and similar in size for

facial emotion recognition (r=.29, p<.01), but nonsignificant for vocal emotion

recognition (r=.12, p=.29). However, facial and vocal emotion recognition (both r>.40,

p<.001) showed positive correlations (i.e., more errors, more of this specific type of

interaction) with n e g a t i ve peer interaction during unstructured interaction with greater

verbal demands, but n e g a t i ve correlations (i.e., more errors, l e s s of this type of

interaction) with low-level peer interaction (both r<-.26, p<.05) during fully unstructured

peer interaction. Vocal emotion recognition correlated positively (i.e., more errors,

more of this type of interaction) with positive peer interaction (r=.29, p<.05) during this

same task. Notably, ToM exhibited a nonsignificant relationship with all social outcomes

in this sample.
